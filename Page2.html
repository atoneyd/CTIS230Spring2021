Good Country People

“Good Country People” takes place in the rural south, and although the exact location is never specified, it would be safe to assume that the setting is O’Connor’s home state of Georgia given the context of her life and her other works. The story also presumably occurs during the 1950s, when it was published. Mrs. Hopewell is content with her life on the farm and enjoys the company of the “good country people” around her. However, she is shown to be quite misguided in her judgments, especially in her view of Manley. The good country people who are “the salt of the earth” are more complex than Mrs. Hopewell makes them out to be (O’Connor 7). Joy, who unlike her mother feels disdain for “good country people,” is also unable to see the signs that she had been lied to by Manley, as her judgment is clouded by her superiority complex. There are many symbols closely related to Joy that paint her to be a vulnerable character and an easy target for Manley’s scheme.

Wooden Leg Symbolism

The wooden leg that Joy wears is an important symbol that appears very often in the story. For Joy, it is something she is dependent on and can’t function normally without it. Her mother views the loss of her original leg as the reason for Joy’s miserable and rude personality. As the thing that ultimately gets taken from her by Manley, the wooden leg is a very prominent symbol in the story. It is something that she is reluctant to show to him, and when she finally does it represents her putting a trust in him that she has never given to anyone else. Her leg is something that makes her vulnerable, shown by her dependence on him once it’s taken off and her inability to escape after he’s stolen it. The stealing of the leg is part of the dramatic shift in the story when Manley reveals that he’s not what he’s made himself out to be. The leg represents Joy’s personality and how she views that she is distinct from the rest of the characters and above them intellectually. It is so integral to her identity that “she took care of it as someone else would his soul” (O’Connor 14). And just as she is physically vulnerable, her intellect is flawed and ultimately contributes to her downfall, as she is deluded by the idea that Manley is just “good country people,” and someone that she can manipulate with ease. She surrenders her wooden leg, and all the time she spent working towards a Ph.D. could not mentally prepare her for what Manley would do.

Glasses Symbolism

While not as important, Joy’s glasses are a symbol that plays a similar role to her wooden leg, as both are items that she uses for support and depends on to go about her daily life. Along with her weak heart and wooden leg, the glasses contribute to Joy’s physical vulnerability, which carries over mentally despite her thinking her mind is superior to others. Manley first takes off her glasses in the barn, showing how he is slowly taking control away from Joy and gaining her trust. Just as he compliments her leg by saying “it’s what makes you different,” he says to Joy earlier in the story, “I like girls that wear glasses” (O’Connor 11). He mentions these things to pretend that he admires her and puts her in a more vulnerable position, while further solidifying that he is “good country people” from her perspective. She is thoroughly deceived by his words and thinks she is “face to face with real innocence,” and believes that he “had touched the truth about her” (O’Connor 14).
