Briefcase Symbolism

Additionally, Manley’s briefcase is representative of his questionable character and what he hides. A first glance at the inside shows the Bibles Manley is supposedly selling, an innocent facade masking his actual intentions. He uses this fake persona of being “just a country boy” to gain the trust of Mrs. Hopewell and, eventually, Joy (O’Connor 7). A closer look into his valise reveals that the Bibles are fake, and inside one of them is “a pocket flask of whiskey, a pack of cards, and a small blue box with printing on it” (O’Connor 15). The briefcase symbolizes the hypocrisy of Manley selling Bibles and claiming to be Christian. The true purpose of the briefcase is revealed along with Manley’s true character, in a dramatic shift in the story where Hulga realizes he is not good country people. When she questions him, saying “aren’t you just good country people?” she murmurs and her voice “had an almost pleading sound” (O’Connor 15). This is a stark contrast to the arrogant and headstrong character that Joy is shown to be at the beginning of the story.

Character Names Symbolism

Another important source of symbolism lies within the names that O’Connor chose for her characters. The character Manley Pointer notably has a double meaning behind his name, being a crude joke that points to his unpleasant true nature, a hint that he is not the nice man that he appears to be. This is also an example of O’Connor’s humor within her writing. We also eventually come to learn that along with the rest of Manley’s persona, his name is fake as well. Mrs. Freeman’s name indicates that there is a sort of freedom in being just “good country people,” instead of being weighed down by the philosophies that Joy studies. There is some irony in the fact that Mrs. Freeman works for Mrs. Hopewell, so in that sense she is not completely free, which may also be a part of O’Connor’s incorporation of humor into the story. Nevertheless, Mrs. Freeman has her own principles and is a free thinker who “could never be brought to admit herself wrong to any point” (O’Connor 2).

There is also Mrs. Hopewell, whose last name points to her hopefulness and trust that good country people are just that. Throughout the story, she is shown to be someone optimistic, who likes to say things like “nothing is perfect” or “that is life” (O’Connor 3) However, she still believes herself to be better than the “good country people” that she adores, evidenced in how she feels she can determine who is really “good country people” and who is “trash” (O’Connor 2).
